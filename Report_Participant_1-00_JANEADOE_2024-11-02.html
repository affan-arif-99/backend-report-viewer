Balanced Nutrition

Dietary patterns characterized by higher intake of vegetables, fruits, fish, nuts, and legumes, and lower intake of meats, high fat dairy, and sweets are related to lower odds of cognitive decline. The place to start is with the eating guidelines in the well-studied MIND diet.

MIND Diet: Recommendations and Progress

The Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurogenerative Delay (MIND) diet is a hybrid of two successful eating plans, but with modifications to reflect the scientific literature on foods and nutrients that are specific to brain health. It emphasizes ten foods that have been shown to slow cognitive decline in clinical studies. It also names five food groups to avoid, due to their adverse effects on the brain.

Your Dietary Recommendations

Start with the eating guidelines in the MIND study. The foods and servings in the “Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay (MIND)” study are further personalized below, using observations from genes, lab results, allergies, medications, and history.[10]

Eating in a way that favors the ApoE-e4 gene focuses on increasing intake of vegetables and fruits, good fats, soluble fiber, lean protein, incorporating probiotic- and prebiotic-rich foods, and drinking green tea.[11] In addition, limit bad fats and foods that have a high glycemic load. The brain thrives on dietary fats, but there is a distinction between good and bad fats.

Enjoy good fats, which include monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats, and medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs).
  • Monounsaturated fats: Include nuts, avocados and avocado oil, extra-virgin olive oil, and olives weekly. Use olive oil for low-heat cooking and coconut oil for baking and high-heat cooking. Avoid canola oil.
  • Polyunsaturated fats: Eat foods rich in omega-3, such as walnuts, ground flaxseed, wild salmon, mackerel (chub or north Atlantic), and sardines, at least three times a week. Omega-3 from food sources is far better absorbed than from taking supplements.
  • MCT oils: Take organic extra-virgin coconut oil, just one tablespoon each day (as it is also a saturated fat).
Cook with fresh turmeric root (or a half-teaspoon of freshly-ground turmeric powder) 3 times a week, or take a 500 mg curcumin supplement twice a day, 5 days a week, as to reduce beta-amyloid oligomerization, to reduce inflammation, to reduce tau aggregation. Assure that any curcumin supplement is formulated with BCM-95® — this supplement is best absorbed by taking it either 1) on an empty stomach, or 2) with one of the good fats described above.

Reduce bad fats, which include saturated fats and trans-fats, as well as seed oils.
  • Saturated fats: Where possible, avoid high-fat dairy items (such as whole milk or whole-fat cheese). Consume animal proteins no more than twice a week. Keep serving sizes palm-sized. If eating beef, focus on grass-fed organic beef. Poultry should be pastured. Fish should be fresh-caught or wild, not farmed. Minimize processed, pre-packaged foods.
  • Trans fats: Avoid foods that contain hydrogenated or partially-hydrogenated oils (like shortening and stick margarine), and decline fried foods. Read the ingredient list in packaged food (verify labels stating “0g trans-fat per serving”).
  • Seed oils, which are heated (as opposed to being cold-pressed as olive oil is), have lost much of their vitamin E due to the heating process, making them synaptic-damaging agents. Avoid heat-processed oils altogether.

Good sources of soluble fiber include: apples, blueberries, carrots, celery, cucumbers, dried peas, chia seeds, flaxseeds, lentils and other beans, nuts, oat bran, oat cereal and oatmeal, oranges, pears, psyllium, and strawberries.

Foods with low-glycemic load have the positive effect of reducing the spike of rapidly-changing insulin levels. So, focus on avoiding food with high glycemic load. Avoid foods and drinks with white sugar, white flour, or high-fructose corn syrup — as well as honey, maple syrup, and molasses. Cut out sugar candies, sweets, and sugary soda. Stick with fist-sized portion sizes (or a half-cup when cut up) of fruits and carbohydrate-rich foods like white rice, white potatoes, corn, and green peas. Aim to keep each food item to a glycemic load of 10 or lower, with a total daily load under 100. Consume a quarter teaspoon of powdered Ceylon cinnamon (cinnamomum verum) 5 days a week, as Glucose is above target, at 96 mg/dL. (Avoid the formulations with Cassia or Saigon cinnamon.)

Among vegetables and fruits, focus on fresh or frozen (plain, without seasoning). Eat three servings of fresh fruit and three-to-five servings of fresh vegetables daily. Over every three days, consume fruits and vegetables from all colors of the rainbow. Limit fruits to one serving per meal or snack, and avoid commercial fruit juices. Rather than making homemade fruit juices, stick with smoothies and include whole fruit, to avoid the high glycemic load of pure juice. For inflammation, eat a teaspoon of freshly-grated ginger rhizome regularly with your meals, or take a 500 mg ginger extract supplement every day, as to reduce inflammation. While the pesticide DDT (which has a toxic metabolite of DDE) has been banned in the USA and Canada since the 1970s, it may still be in use in other countries. Studies have shown that AD risk increases for those with both ApoE4 and elevated serum levels of DDE. Minimize eating any fruits or vegetables grown outside of the USA or Canada unless they are organic. The EWG organization maintains two lists titled the Dirty Dozen and the Clean Fifteen. Fruits and vegetables in the Dirty Dozen list should be always purchased in organic form; foods in the Clean Fifteen need not be organic (if grown in the USA or Canada). https://www.ewg.org/foodnews/summary.php

Lean protein can improve satiety and blood sugar regulation, and should be part of each meal and snack. Additionally, one's ability to build muscle mass deteriorates as they age, so protein requirements increase even as intake and appetite may wane. Supplementing with protein powder can increase lean body mass and muscle. Mix 20 to 30 grams of protein powder into a daily shake. Consume beans as an alternative protein source weekly. Good dietary sources of lean protein include: grass-fed organic beef or bison, low-fat dairy products, eggs, fish (free of mercury), nuts, organic poultry, seeds, soy products (such as tofu and tempeh), and beans and legumes.

Prebiotics and probiotics: help maintain a healthy immune system and digestive health. While food sources are the preferred choice, recommendations for high-quality probiotic supplementation are Lactobacillus acidophilus and Bifidobacterium lactis at a dose of 4 to 8 billion colonies, one to three times a day. Among probiotic food sources, consider: live cultures in dairy products (such as plain yogurt, buttermilk, & kefir), tempeh, miso soup, natto, kimchi, and freshly-made pickles or sauerkraut. Prebiotics are non-digestible fiber compounds in foods that promote the growth and activity of beneficial microorganisms in the gut. Good prebiotic sources include: asparagus, under-ripe bananas, flaxseeds, garlic, Jerusalem artichoke, jicama, leafy greens, leeks, legumes, and onions. The word prebiotic is seldom used on the label, so look for: galactooligosaccharides (GOS), fructooligosaccharides (FOS), oligofructose (OF), chicory fiber, or inulin. Discuss specific recommendations with a dietitian.

Epidemiologic studies have identified coffee and green tea as protective against cognitive impairment, as well as having antioxidant properties. Slow caffeine metabolism is indicated by the CYP1A2 genes, so drink no more than two cups of coffee each day (200mg of caffeine) and finish them by noon. Although there is caffeine in green tea, it contains less caffeine than black tea, and in turn black tea has less caffeine than coffee. Wait two hours after taking the medication levothyroxine before drinking any coffee. Avoid using coffee as a way to introduce hidden sugar into your daily intake.

Weight loss is essential, targeting a body mass index (BMI) under 25 over time.[12,13] Your genes indicate that you are part of the 39% of the population who might find they lose 2.5 times as much weight on a low-fat diet (as opposed to a low-carb diet).

Homocysteine Concerns
High homocysteine 13 µmol/L
target: < 7.0 µmol/L
This level of homocysteine is above target. Elevated levels are associated with atrophy of a key area in the brain for memory, called the hippocampus. High homocysteine can also facilitate a buildup of undesirable components in the brain, known as beta-amyloid plaques and tau proteins. Without adequate intake of the right B vitamins, homocysteine can accumulate and contribute to memory problems.
Very low vitamin B12 139 pg/mL
target: ≥ 500 pg/mL
This very low vitamin B12 level may be linked to the break down of myelin (the protective sheath around nerve cells), brain cell atrophy, increased inflammation, and high homocysteine levels. All of these can contribute to memory problems. Further evaluation as to its cause is recommended.
Insulin Concerns
Very high insulin 15.7 µIU/mL
target: < 4.5 µIU/mL
Elevated insulin levels, including the high insulin associated with type 2 diabetes, are associated with key drivers of cognitive decline: brain inflammation, amyloid plaques, and tau tangles. In particular, high insulin increases beta-amyloid accumulation. This insulin level, far above target, may significantly accelerate the progression of cognitive decline, so further evaluation is recommended.
Very high HOMA-IR 3.7
target: < 1.9
The formula known as the Homeostatic Model Assessment for Insulin Resistance (HOMA-IR) estimates insulin resistance using your fasting glucose and insulin levels. The high level seen here can occur when your body cannot readily absorb glucose from the bloodstream. The body thus needs higher levels of insulin to help glucose enter cells. Insulin resistance may contribute to fatigue, and can lead to brain inflammation, amyloid plaques, and tau tangles, so further evaluation is recommended.

target: < 91 mg/dL
A level of 100 to 125 mg/dL indicates impaired fasting glucose, a type of prediabetes. This increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes. Insulin resistance can lead to brain inflammation, amyloid plaques, and tau tangles — each a driver of cognitive decline.
Cholesterol Imbalance

Abnormal Thyroid Function
Moderately high thyroid stimulating hormone 3.0 µIU/mL
target: < 2.2 µIU/mL
Thyroid hormone balance is important for synaptic balance and cognitive optimization. Hypothyroidism (as indicated by analyzing TSH, T3 and T4 levels) are known to cause cognitive impairment. Low free T3 2.3 pg/mL

Thyroid hormone balance is important for synaptic balance and cognitive optimization. The thyroid produces “inactive” thyroxine (T4), which is converted into “active” triiodothyronine (T3). T3 helps regulate the body's temperature, metabolism, and heart rate. The T3 that doesn't otherwise bind to protein is called free T3. Higher levels of free T3 are desirable to prevent cognitive decline.
Essential Metals Imbalance
Low zinc 71 µg/dL
target: ≥ 90 µg/dL
Within the brain, zinc plays a key role in reducing inflammation. Brain inflammation is often seen in those with cognitive decline, and may directly impact its progression. This low level may also be an indicator of metal toxicity, such as elevated levels of mercury or lead in the blood.
Very high copper/zinc ratio 1.7
target: < 1.2
This ratio between copper and zinc is far above target. High copper is linked to a decline of cognitive abilities, whereas zinc is neuroprotective and has been shown to reduce inflammation and to lower copper levels. The relationship between these two is out of balance; a target ratio is 1.0 (that is, approximately one-to-one), each with a value near 100.
Vitamin Imbalance
Low vitamin D 24.8 ng/mL
target: ≥ 55 ng/mL
Those with low vitamin D levels may lose memory and thinking abilities faster than those with optimal levels. Vitamin D affects bone metabolism, reduces inflammation, appears to inhibit the development of Alzheimer's disease, and affects cardiovascular disease.
Kidney Function
Low eGFR 51 mL/min
target: ≥ 60 mL/min
This value for estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) is low, indicating possible chronic kidney disease (CKD). eGFR signals how well kidneys are filtering blood and getting rid of waste. A normal reading for eGFR ranges from 90 to 130 ml/min, with no protein in the urine. A reading of 60 to 89 may indicate a mild decrease in kidney function. Values under 60 imply stage 3 CKD.
Low creatinine clearance 48.1 mL/min
target: ≥ 60 mL/min
Creatinine clearance (CrCl) is a measure of the flow rate through the kidneys, and that rate is below target. This indicates possible stage 3 chronic kidney disease (CKD). The Gault-Cockcroft equation is used to calculate creatinine clearance. It takes four values: 1) a person's blood creatinine level, 2) their age, 3) their gender, and 4) their body weight (unlike eGFR, where weight is not a component). Creatinine clearance provides a repeatable measure of renal filtering capability which takes all of these factors into account.

STOP-Bang Risk Index for Obstructive Sleep Apnea

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)[14] is the most prevalent sleep-breathing disturbance, affecting about 34% of men and 17% of women. A sleep apnea episode is a cessation of breathing (apnea) or a marked reduction in airflow (hypopnea) during sleep. An episode is considered clinically relevant if it lasts more than 10 seconds; episodes of apneas and hypopneas often persist for 30-60 seconds. It is estimated that the average life span of someone with untreated OSA is 58 years.

The STOP-Bang index[15,16] estimates the risk for OSA as follows:

  • High risk:
    • 5 or more Yes answers, or

    • 2 or more Yes answers to the STOP questions and Yes for 'N', or
    • 2 or more Yes answers to the STOP questions and Yes for 'G'
